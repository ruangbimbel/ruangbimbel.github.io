Language Features in the Narrative Text "Hansel and Gretel":

1. Past Tense Verbs: The story is narrated in the past tense, describing events that happened in the past, such as "lived," "told," "abandoned," "realized," "came," "prepared," and "coaxed."

2. Descriptive Language: The text uses descriptive language to paint a vivid picture of the setting and characters. For example, "a very, very poor woodcutter," "a pretty little house," and "made of candy, cakes, and chocolate."

3. Dialogue: The text includes dialogue to show conversations between the characters, such as the conversation between the stepmother and the woodcutter, and the witch and the children.

4. Time Connectors: Time connectors are used to show the sequence of events, such as "Once upon a time," "One day," "That night," "Days went by," and "At home."

5. Adverbs of Frequency: Adverbs of frequency are used to describe how often something happens, such as "very," "so," and "suddenly."

6. Direct Speech: The characters' words are presented in direct speech, using quotation marks to indicate their speech, for example, "Isn't this nice?" and "What's going on here? Who is eating my house?"

7. Descriptive Adjectives: The text includes descriptive adjectives to provide more details about the characters and objects, such as "wicked witch," "pretty little house," "vast treasure," and "ungodly witch."

8. Moral Lesson: Narrative texts often contain a moral lesson or message. In this story, it teaches the importance of being resourceful and clever in challenging situations.

9. Sequence of Events: The text follows a clear sequence of events, starting with the children being abandoned in the forest, finding the candy house, getting trapped by the witch, and finally defeating the witch and returning home.

10. Happy Ending: Many narrative texts have a happy ending, and this story is no exception, as it concludes with the family reunited and living happily ever after.
